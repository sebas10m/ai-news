The Founding Fathers and Modern America

Chapter 1. The Constitutional Framework Under Modern Strain

The Founders conceived a constitutional order as a delicate balance of power among three branches of government. They believed that each branch, the executive, the legislative, and the judiciary, would act as a natural restraint on the others.

In the digital age, the founders would likely view the nation's surveillance programs as a great challenge. They would likely demand a return to first principles, robust legal scrutiny for surveillance actions, and a firm commitment to warrants and due process before delving into personal data.

Chapter 4. Economic Power and Corporate Influence

The founders would have been amazed by the scale and complexity of modern corporate power. They would have questioned the boundaries that separate the public and private spheres, and would have advocated for a more transparent and accountable system.

Chapter 6. Education, Citizenship, and Civic Virtue

The founders would likely have been disappointed by the reduction of education to private gain rather than public good. They would have encouraged educators to introduce students not simply to the mechanics of government, but to the broader principles of republican self-rule.

Chapter 7. Religion, Secularism, and the Public Sphere

The founders would have seen the rise of secularism and the complex interplay between religion and public policy. They would have advised modern Americans to reinforce their faith in the world, and to maintain a shared civic identity.

Chapter 9. Technological Advancement and Democratic Discourse

The founders would have viewed the digital era's distortions of discourse with great concern. They would have advocated for a new ethic of communication, demanding greater transparency from the political system and the platforms that shape public opinion.

Chapter 10. Renewing the American Experiment

The founders would have encouraged a new world of freedom, revisiting the Fourth Amendment with fresh eyes, establishing stronger watchdog institutions, and empowering citizens through clear laws that define where and how the state may tread in their personal domains.

They would have insisted that the republic's future depends on remembering that democracy is not a state of rest, but a constant endeavor. Just as they built an enduring framework out of fierce debates and thoughtful compromise, they would have urged their successors to combine the wisdom of the past with the inventive energy of the present.
